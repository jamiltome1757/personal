Mathematics

A quadratic equation is an equation where the highest index of the variable is squared. Quadratic equations also refer to any equation that can be rearranged into the standard form.

Standard Form ( Or General Form): ax² + bx + c

In the standard form of the quadratic equation, a, b, and c are known values and a cannot be 0. If a is 0, the equation will become linear, not quadratic. p

However, not all quadratic equations look like the standard form. Some equations require expanding and moving terms to resemble the standard form.

For instance, upon first glance, x(x-1) = 2 may not look like a quadratic equation, but once it is expanded, and 2 is moved to the left, it becomes x² - x - 2 (a = 1, b = -1, c = -2).

Another example is (x+2)(x+4). Once the expression is expanded through the foil method, it becomes x² + 6x + 8 (a = 1, b = 6, c = 8).

Other forms of the quadratic equations include:

Intercept Form: a(x-p)(x-q)

where p and q are the zeroes (roots) of the equation.

Vertex Form (Completing the square): a(x-h)² +k

where the coordinate (h, k) is the vertex of the parabola.

Parabolas

Quadratic functions form graphs called "parabolas". Parabolas are vaguely u - shaped, and will have a highest or a lowest point called the vertex. Parabolas may open up or down depending on whether the leading coefficient 'a' is positive or negative.

Solving Quadratic Equations

There are many ways to solve quadratic equations. A way is to plug in the known coefficients a, b, and c of the standard form into the quadratic formula which is x = (-b±√(b²-4ac))/(2a).

Another method of solving quadratic equations is to factorise the polynomial.
